## Packet Analysis with Wireshark

This month focused on network security. We spent significant time in Wireshark capturing and analyzing live traffic — filtering by protocol, following TCP streams, and identifying anomalies. In one lab, we had to find a plaintext password being sent over an unencrypted HTTP connection buried in a 20-minute packet capture. Scrolling through raw packets until something clicks is tedious, but it taught me more about how protocols actually behave than any textbook.

## Network Fundamentals

We converted between binary, decimal, and hexadecimal representations of IP addresses, mapped out subnets, and diagrammed network topologies. Understanding CIDR notation and how subnet masks determine broadcast domains made later exercises on firewall rules much easier to reason about.

## Linux Process Management and Password Auditing

On the system side, we managed Linux processes — using `ps`, `top`, and `kill` to monitor and control what's running. The password auditing lab was eye-opening: we used John the Ripper against a shadow file of hashed passwords. Simple passwords fell in seconds. Dictionary words with a number appended lasted maybe a minute. It was a concrete demonstration of why password policies exist.

## Takeaway

The biggest lesson this month was how much information travels in the clear if you know where to look. Even on supposedly secure networks, misconfigurations leak data constantly.
