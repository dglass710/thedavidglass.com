## Packet Analysis with Wireshark

I spent most of this month on network security. I worked extensively in Wireshark, filtering by protocol, following TCP streams, and identifying anomalies in live traffic. In one lab, I had to find a plaintext password being sent over an unencrypted HTTP connection buried in a 20-minute packet capture. Scrolling through raw packets until something clicks is tedious, but it taught me more about how protocols actually behave than any textbook.

## Network Fundamentals

I converted between binary, decimal, and hexadecimal representations of IP addresses, mapped out subnets, and diagrammed network topologies. Understanding CIDR notation and how subnet masks determine broadcast domains made later exercises on firewall rules much easier to reason about.

## Linux Process Management and Password Auditing

On the system side, I managed Linux processes — using `ps`, `top`, and `kill` to monitor and control what's running. I used John the Ripper against a shadow file of hashed passwords. John cracked simple passwords in seconds. Dictionary words with a number appended lasted maybe a minute.

## Takeaway

The biggest lesson this month was how much information travels in the clear if you know where to look. That plaintext password sitting in an HTTP stream during the Wireshark lab wasn't a contrived example — unencrypted services on internal networks are still common enough to find without trying.
