## DHCP, NAT, and Routing

One lab had us deliberately misconfigure a router and then diagnose why two subnets couldn't communicate — working backward from a broken state was more instructive than building it correctly the first time. I also traced how DHCP assigns addresses, how NAT translates private IPs to public ones, and how routing tables determine where packets go.

## Wireless Security with Aircrack-ng

I used Aircrack-ng to capture a WPA2 handshake from a test access point and then ran a dictionary attack against it. The tool cracked the weak passphrase in under a minute. I then repeated the exercise with a strong, random passphrase — and watched it fail after running through the entire wordlist.

## Packet Analysis, Continued

I also went deeper into packet analysis, examining how OSPF advertises network paths and how an attacker could inject false routes. These protocols were designed for cooperative networks, not adversarial ones — which is why injecting false routes still works.

## Takeaway

The thing that surprised me most this month was how many network protocols assume good faith. OSPF, for instance, trusts any router that says "I have a shorter path" — a design decision from the 1980s that still shapes how I have to think about network defense today.
