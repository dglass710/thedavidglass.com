## DHCP, NAT, and Routing

Month three moved into how networks actually operate at scale. We traced how DHCP assigns addresses, how NAT translates private IPs to public ones, and how routing tables determine where packets go. One lab had us deliberately misconfigure a router and then diagnose why two subnets couldn't communicate — working backward from a broken state was more instructive than building it correctly the first time.

## Wireless Security with Aircrack-ng

The wireless security module was the highlight. We used Aircrack-ng to capture a WPA2 handshake from a test access point and then ran a dictionary attack against it. The tool cracked the weak passphrase in under a minute. We then repeated the exercise with a strong, random passphrase — and watched it fail after running through the entire wordlist. Simple demonstration, but it made the difference between weak and strong credentials visceral rather than abstract.

## Packet Analysis, Continued

We also went deeper into packet analysis, examining how routing protocols like OSPF advertise network paths and how an attacker could inject false routes. Understanding the trust assumptions baked into these protocols — they were designed for cooperative networks, not adversarial ones — explained a lot about why network attacks are still so effective.

## Takeaway

The thing that surprised me most this month was how many network protocols assume good faith. Security often means compensating for design decisions made decades ago.
