## Reconnaissance with Shodan

This month shifted to offensive security, and the intensity jumped. We started with reconnaissance — using Shodan to find internet-facing devices with known vulnerabilities. Searching for unpatched services on specific ports and seeing real, exposed systems was sobering. It made clear how much of the attack surface is just forgotten infrastructure that nobody turned off.

## The MITRE ATT&CK Framework

We studied the MITRE ATT&CK framework as a way to map adversary behavior. Instead of thinking about attacks as isolated events, MITRE organizes them into tactics (what the attacker wants) and techniques (how they do it). We used it to diagram a multi-stage attack from initial access through data exfiltration, which forced us to think about how defenders need to cover every stage — attackers only need to find one gap.

## Exploitation with Metasploit

The Metasploit labs were where everything came together. We ran exploits against intentionally vulnerable VMs — scanning for open services, selecting the right module, configuring payloads, and gaining shell access. In one exercise, I exploited an outdated Samba service on a Linux target to get a reverse shell in about three commands. Knowing how fast that can happen changes how you think about patch management.

## Takeaway

Offensive security is where I want to focus. Understanding the attacker's workflow — recon, enumeration, exploitation, persistence — gives you a clearer picture of what actually needs defending than any compliance checklist.
