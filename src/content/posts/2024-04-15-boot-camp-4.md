## Reconnaissance with Shodan

I shifted to offensive security this month, and the exercises required chaining multiple tools together. I started with reconnaissance — using Shodan to find internet-facing devices with known vulnerabilities. Searching for unpatched SMB services on port 445 and finding hundreds of real, exposed systems — some running Windows Server 2008 — made clear how much of the attack surface is just forgotten infrastructure that nobody turned off.

## The MITRE ATT&CK Framework

I studied the MITRE ATT&CK framework as a way to map adversary behavior. I used it to diagram a multi-stage attack from initial access through data exfiltration. Mapping each step to specific technique IDs showed me how many detection opportunities exist between the initial foothold and the actual damage — and how most orgs only monitor for the last one.

## Exploitation with Metasploit

In the Metasploit labs, I ran exploits against intentionally vulnerable VMs — scanning for open services, selecting the right module, configuring payloads, and gaining shell access. In one exercise, I exploited an outdated Samba service on a Linux target to get a reverse shell in about three commands. Knowing how fast that can happen changes how you think about patch management.

## Takeaway

Offensive security is where I want to focus. The Samba exploit took three commands and maybe ninety seconds from scan to shell — that's what convinced me that defense that ignores the attacker's perspective has blind spots.
